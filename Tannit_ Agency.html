(01) The Problem

Heavy brand.
Light promise.

Tannit is one vendor for design, production, and delivery, but the identity didn't read that way. Dense illustrations, muted colours, and layered visuals that signalled complexity rather than capability.

Three things had to happen at once: reduce the visual noise, protect what already worked (the triangle, the tone, the equity the founders had built), and create one system that could speak to two completely different audiences.

(04) The Palette

Two colours.
One clear voice.

The original palette had eight or more colours with no hierarchy. The new system leads with two: a deep teal and a muted violet. Warm white and near-black complete the set — the kind of neutral ground that lets the two main colours do the talking.

(06) Illustration

Two delivered.
A third on the way.

Two illustrations, each with a colour and a monochrome version. Clean linework, system colours only, sized to work across decks and one-pagers without adding visual weight.

One honest note. A third illustration is taking longer than expected. Past a certain point, forcing it beyond your current skill produces less than bringing in a specialist. A system built around transferable files outlasts whoever made it.

08

What I'd do differently
with the same brief.

I spent real hours animating illustrations: loops for social, motion variants for decks. Tannit barely uses either format. The work was technically solid and completely beside the point. Deliverables should follow how the client actually communicates.

The founders still build every pitch deck and email from scratch. A reusable template would have delivered more value per hour than any animation I exported. That's where the system should have started.

The illustrations follow the same pattern. Two pieces are in use; a third was planned and never delivered because it depended entirely on my availability. Files that any designer can open and extend are worth more to the client than files that require a call to me first.

Two founders, three women, one brief over dinner.
Tannit left with a system they can use on their own and a brand that says what they actually do.

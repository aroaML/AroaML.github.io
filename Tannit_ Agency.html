(01) The Problem

Heavy brand.
Light promise.

Tannit is one vendor for design, production, and delivery. The identity didn't reflect that. Dense illustrations, muted colors, visual layers that said a lot without landing anywhere.

Three things had to happen at once: clean up the noise, protect what already felt right (the triangle, the tone, the earned equity), and build one system that could speak to two completely different audiences.

(03) The Symbol

One fixed point:
the triangle.

The symbol references the Phoenician goddess Tanit, the one element the founders chose from the start. A second inner triangle and a diagonal purple line came later, breaking symmetry and encoding the three women behind the agency into the mark.

(06) Illustration

Two delivered.
A third on the way.

Two illustrations, each with a color and monochrome variant: clean lines, palette restricted to system colors, placed with intention across decks and one-pagers.

One honest note. A third illustration is taking longer than expected. Past a certain point, forcing it beyond your current skill produces less than bringing in a specialist. A system built around transferable files outlasts whoever made it.

(07) The Brand, Applied

The real test:
the founders use it on their own.

The pitch deck has a fixed structure and swappable content. The founders update cases and numbers each time, the layout stays. One grid, same typefaces and colors, two completely different conversations.

08

Where the time went,
and where it should have gone.

I spent real hours building animated versions of the illustrations, for social media and presentations. Tannit generates few contexts for either, and none of that production time turned into something the founders actually use.

Those hours would have gone further into presentation and email templates, tools the founders still use every week without a clean system. Next time: templates before animation.

The same applies to illustration. Knowing when to bring in a specialist is a design decision too. A system built around one person's availability has a shelf life. Transferable files outlast whoever made them.

Tannit left with a full toolkit, digital brand guidelines, and a clearer picture of who they are visually.
